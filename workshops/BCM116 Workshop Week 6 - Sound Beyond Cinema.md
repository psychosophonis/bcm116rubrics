## BCM116 Workshop Week 6 - Sound Beyond Cinema.

In the lecture I spoke about two immersive media examples that, not only do I love, but offer some really interesting ways of using audio to structure interaction in the production of an immersive media experience; **Blast Theory's** wonderful multipart audio experiences and **Cardiff and Miller's** 'Physical Cinema' work Alter Banhoff Video Walk.

These are pretty simple ideas- technically speaking - but they require you to work with audio at quite a high technical standard. To enable this kind of work I want to give you the skills you need to mix and process stereo audio and to give you the option of working with Dolby Atmos.

#### Today I want to cover some basic audio processing for dialogue/voice that all screen media project should use:

**Compression**: Ensuring your voice recording a punchy, full bodied and able to site forward in the mix.

**Equalisation:** Ensuring the most important part of the voice cuts through the mix and the bits that can cause noise or muddiness are removed. 

**Reverb:** We know what it is but using it well is hard.  In a mic it can act as  the glue that help everything sit together a little better....if we send multiple sounds to one reverb we are effectively putting them in the same space.... It could go in each of these sections as its used to process dialogue, to create a sense of space, and to help things sit in a mix.

#### Then we want to talk about the most basic aspect of mixing.

**Volume:**  This one is pretty obvious - we all know what its is - but in mixing we need to make sure we place sounds carefully so they find a place in the mix from front to back. Things in the back are still getting heard...they just don't need to be placed at equal footing at the front of the mix. 

**Pan:** If volume is about finding a place in the mix from front to back, panning allows us to do the same left to right (in stereo). Important elements like dialogue will always be centre...but perhaps we can make more room for that centre by moving less important sounds left and/or right as well as back. 

#### **Then we want to talk about Spatial Audio and Stereo**

**Stereo**: We have two ears. This provides a sense of position to objects we hear.  When we hear a sound in both sounds at equal level in the same ear it sounds like one sound coming from directly in front of me - or if I'm wearing headphones form inside my head. 

**Temporal Fusion/Precedence:**  When I hear a sound in one ear followed by the same sound in the other ear and they are within about 50ms of each other they will like one event (they will fuse) coming from the direction of the first sound. 

**Hacking it:** Hacking the qualities above allows us to create rich stereo sound scapes.

***Try This:***  If we have two different sounds that share some qualities or content - say two lengths of atmos we can pan one track left and the other right and the things they share will meet in the middle.... This creates expansive sound scapes. 

***Try This:*** If we have copy a sound to two tracks and displace it in time slightly and then pan it left and right we can give it dimension and interest.

**Reverb:** Use reverb with subtltey to help glue this all together and give it place it in a simulated space.

#### **Some More Advanced Notes:**

Mixing well is about giving every element in the mix a clear position in (for stereo) a three dimensional field. **Volume** is the position of an object front to back, **pan** is left to right, **frequency** content is the 'vertical' axis. **Think of a drum kit** - it has a specific drum to fit in each frequency band width - the snare cuts through in the mid range, the kick drum sits low, the hi hat an cymbals in the  symbols sit up high, the Toms sit between the kick and the snare. Everything has its place. This is how you should think about mixing. Clever people make sure no sound gets anywhere near the dialogue on anny axis... Volume, Pan, Frequency.
