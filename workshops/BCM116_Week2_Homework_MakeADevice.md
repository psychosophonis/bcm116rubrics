# Homework: Make a Spinning Disc, Thaumatrope, or Flipbook

No fixed pipeline. Pick a starting point, follow it, cross into another process wherever it's useful. The toolkit below is a menu, not a sequence.

## By hand

Pens, card, compass, scissors. Draw your frames directly, cut, assemble, spin. The original process — still the fastest way to just try something.

## Video → numbered frames (Photoshop)

1. **File > Import > Video Frames to Layers.** Choose "Selected Range Only" and set a frame limit — trim to roughly the number of divisions your disc or flipbook needs before importing, not after.
2. Each frame is now a layer, in order.
3. **File > Scripts > Export Layers to Files** — set a folder, name prefix, JPG/PNG. This numbers them automatically, ready for the generators below.

**Intervene before exporting:** with your frames as layers, you can draw or paint directly onto individual layers — scratch marks, added text, colour shifts — before they ever leave Photoshop. A few frames altered, most left alone, is often more interesting than treating every frame the same.

## Disc / flipbook generator

Drop your numbered folder in. Sets registration point, crop, and layout. Produces a print-ready PDF, or a full-disc preview image on screen if you're not printing.

**Intervene after generating:**
- Draw directly onto the physical or exported sheet before cutting — hand marks over machine layout.
- Re-photograph or re-scan the printed/cut result as a new set of frames to run through again.
- Cut a figure out of each frame and collage them — pull the subject out of its sequence entirely and build something new from the pieces.

## PhenaSpin (disc image → spinning video)

Upload a disc image — your generator's full-disc preview, or a historical disc you've found — and set frame count to match its divisions. Plays back as a looping video, no printing required.

**Intervene:** feed it a disc you've drawn or scratched by hand and scanned, not just a digitally generated one.

## Crossing over, generally

The interesting move is usually the handoff, not the tool: draw something, photograph it, run it through a generator, print it, draw on the print, photograph that. Where you stop is up to you — one pass or several.
